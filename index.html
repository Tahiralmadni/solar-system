The Sun

The star at the center of our Solar System. It is a nearly perfect sphere of hot plasma, heated to incandescence by nuclear fusion reactions in its core.

  • Massive Scale: The Sun accounts for 99.86% of the total mass of the entire Solar System.
  • Nuclear Powerhouse: Every second, it fuses about 600 million tons of hydrogen into helium.

Mercury

The smallest planet in the Solar System and the closest to the Sun. Its surface is heavily cratered and similar in appearance to the Moon.

  • Extreme Temperatures: Despite being closest to the Sun, it's not the hottest planet, but has extreme temperature swings from -173°C to 427°C.
  • Short Year: A year on Mercury takes only 88 Earth days.

Venus

The second planet from the Sun. It has a dense atmosphere of carbon dioxide that traps heat, making it the hottest planet in our solar system.

  • Backward Spin: Venus rotates in the opposite direction to most other planets (retrograde rotation).
  • Long Day: A day on Venus is longer than a year on Venus.

Earth

Our home. The third planet from the Sun and the only astronomical object known to harbor life. It has a liquid water surface and a protective atmosphere.

  • Blue Marble: About 71% of Earth's surface is covered with water.
  • Dynamic Planet: Earth is the only planet in our solar system with active plate tectonics.

Mars

The fourth planet from the Sun, often called the "Red Planet" due to reddish iron oxide prevalent on its surface.

  • Giant Volcano: Mars is home to Olympus Mons, the largest volcano in the solar system, three times the height of Everest.
  • Dust Storms: It has the largest dust storms in the solar system, capable of covering the entire planet for months.

Jupiter

The largest planet in the Solar System. It is a gas giant with a mass one-thousandth that of the Sun, but two-and-a-half times that of all the other planets combined.

  • The Great Red Spot: A giant storm bigger than Earth that has raged for hundreds of years.
  • Many Moons: Jupiter has 95 known moons, including the four large Galilean moons.

Saturn

The sixth planet from the Sun and the second-largest. It is most famous for its stunning ring system, composed mainly of ice particles with a smaller amount of rocky debris.

  • Low Density: Saturn is the only planet in our solar system that is less dense than water.
  • Hexagon Storm: There is a persisting hexagonal cloud pattern around the north pole.

Uranus

The seventh planet from the Sun. It has the third-largest planetary radius and fourth-largest planetary mass in the Solar System.

  • Tilted Giant: Uranus rotates at a nearly 90-degree angle from the plane of its orbit, appearing to roll on its side.
  • Ice Giant: Its atmosphere is composed of hydrogen and helium, but it contains more "ices" like water, ammonia, and methane.

Neptune

The eighth and farthest-known Solar planet from the Sun. It is dark, cold, and whipped by supersonic winds.

  • Fastest Winds: Winds on Neptune can reach up to 2,100 km/h (1,300 mph).
  • Mathematical Discovery: Neptune was the first planet located through mathematical calculations rather than observation.
